  \section{Introduction \& Motivation}

  My fascination with natural language processing began during my undergraduate years at IIT Delhi, where I built a cross-lingual named entity recognition system for low-resource Indic languages. The challenge of enabling machines to understand the rich linguistic diversity of South Asia — where over 1,200 languages are spoken yet fewer than thirty have meaningful NLP support — shaped my research philosophy: technology must bridge linguistic divides, not widen them.

  This conviction led me to pursue deeper questions about how large language models represent and transfer knowledge across languages. I am drawn to the tension between the remarkable zero-shot capabilities of modern LLMs and their uneven performance on non-English tasks, particularly for morphologically complex languages like Tamil and Telugu.

  \section{Research Experience}

  At the Language Technologies Research Centre at IIIT Hyderabad, under the supervision of Dr.\ Ramesh Krishnan, I investigated cross-lingual transfer learning for semantic parsing. We developed a novel data augmentation technique — Anchor-Token Perturbation — that improves zero-shot transfer to target languages by selectively masking syntactically salient tokens during fine-tuning. Our system achieved a 12\% relative improvement over mBERT baselines on the XNLI benchmark for Hindi, Tamil, and Telugu. This work was accepted at EMNLP 2024.

  I also spent a summer at Nexa Research India, contributing to the IndicTrans2 machine translation project. Working alongside the MT team, I built evaluation pipelines for the 22 scheduled Indian languages and co-authored a technical report on quality disparities between high-resource and low-resource language pairs. That experience impressed upon me the importance of rigorous evaluation — how subtle annotation artifacts can inflate reported metrics and mask real-world failures.

  \section{Why Stanford}

  Stanford's NLP Group represents precisely the intellectual environment I seek. Professor Christopher Manning's foundational work on linguistic structure and neural networks, and Professor Percy Liang's research on robust and trustworthy language models align directly with my interests in cross-lingual generalization and evaluation. I am particularly excited by the group's emphasis on connecting theoretical insights in linguistics with empirical advances in deep learning — a synthesis I believe essential for the next generation of multilingual systems.

  Beyond research, I am drawn to Stanford's culture of interdisciplinary collaboration. The intersection of NLP with human--computer interaction at the Stanford HCI Group presents opportunities I am eager to explore — particularly around building assistive language tools for speakers of underserved languages.

  \section{Future Directions}

  During my Ph.D., I aim to develop methods that make large language models genuinely multilingual — not by scaling data collection alone, but by designing architectures and training objectives that encode linguistic structure more efficiently. I want to understand \textit{why} certain typological features resist cross-lingual transfer and build systems that adapt gracefully to languages with limited digital resources.

  In the long term, I aspire to lead a research lab that bridges academic NLP research with practical deployment for linguistic communities in South Asia and beyond. Democratizing language technology is, I believe, one of the most impactful contributions a researcher can make.

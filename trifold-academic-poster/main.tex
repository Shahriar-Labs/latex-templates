  \paneltitle{\faFile\hspace{4pt}Abstract \& Background}

  \smallsep

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  Early detection of wildfires is critical for minimizing
  environmental damage and protecting vulnerable communities.
  Traditional detection methods relying on human observation or
  ground-based sensor networks suffer from limited coverage and
  significant response delays. This study presents a novel deep
  transfer learning framework that leverages multi-spectral
  satellite imagery for real-time wildfire detection at a
  global scale.}

  \tinysep

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  Our approach combines pre-trained convolutional neural
  networks with spatial-channel attention mechanisms and
  domain-specific fine-tuning on a curated dataset of over
  50,000 satellite images spanning five continents and seven
  distinct climate zones. The framework achieves 94.7\%
  detection accuracy with a mean detection latency of
  3.2 minutes when deployed on edge computing hardware.}

  \smallsep

  \subheading{Motivation}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  Climate change has intensified wildfire frequency and
  severity worldwide. In 2024 alone, wildfires burned over
  12 million hectares globally, emitting an estimated
  1.8 gigatons of CO\textsubscript{2}. Current satellite-based
  detection systems, such as MODIS and VIIRS, provide valuable
  data but suffer from latency issues and limited spatial
  resolution, often detecting fires only after they have
  grown to substantial sizes.}

  \smallsep

  \subheading{Research Gap}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  While deep learning has shown promise in remote sensing
  applications, existing models are typically trained on
  region-specific data and fail to generalize across diverse
  ecosystems. Furthermore, most approaches require cloud-based
  inference pipelines, introducing unacceptable latency for
  time-critical wildfire response scenarios.}

  \smallsep

  \begin{highlightbox}
    {\footnotesize\bfseries\color{primary}Key Challenge:}
    {\footnotesize\color{darktext} Can we build a lightweight,
    globally-generalizable deep learning model that detects
    wildfires within minutes using standard satellite imagery,
    while running on edge devices at ground stations?}
  \end{highlightbox}

  \paneltitle{\faCogs\hspace{4pt}Methodology}

  \smallsep

  \subheading{Dataset: WildFire-Net}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  We curated a dataset of 50,000 multi-spectral image patches
  (512\,$\times$\,512 pixels) from Sentinel-2 and Landsat-8
  satellites. Images span 2019--2025 and cover boreal forests
  (Canada, Siberia), Mediterranean shrublands (Spain, Greece),
  tropical savannas (Brazil, Australia), temperate forests
  (California, Portugal), and arid grasslands (Central Asia).
  Each patch is labeled with a binary fire/non-fire mask
  verified by expert annotators.}

  \smallsep

  \subheading{Model Architecture}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  Our framework, named \textbf{WildFire-Net}, builds on
  EfficientNet-B4 pre-trained on ImageNet-21K. We augment the
  backbone with:}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Spatial Attention Module:} 3\,$\times$\,3
      depthwise-separable convolutions with dilated kernels
      (rates = 1, 2, 3) to capture multi-scale smoke patterns.
    \item \textbf{Channel Attention Module:} Squeeze-and-
      excitation blocks recalibrating spectral band weights,
      emphasizing near-infrared (B8) and shortwave-infrared
      (B11, B12) bands critical for fire detection.
    \item \textbf{Lightweight Decoder:} A single upsampling
      block with skip connections, reducing parameters by
      40\% compared to U-Net-style decoders.
  \end{itemize}
  }

  \smallsep

  \subheading{Training Protocol}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  Two-stage training on 4\,$\times$\,NVIDIA A100 GPUs:}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  \textbf{Stage 1 --- Pre-training:} ImageNet-21K weights
  fine-tuned on the BigEarthNet-MM multi-spectral benchmark
  (590K Sentinel patches, 43 land-cover classes) for 50 epochs.
  Learning rate: 1e-4 with cosine annealing.}

  \tinysep

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  \textbf{Stage 2 --- Domain Adaptation:} Fine-tuned on
  WildFire-Net with a weighted focal loss ($\gamma = 2.0$,
  $\alpha = 0.75$ to address class imbalance). Data
  augmentation includes random rotations, flips, spectral
  jitter, and CutMix. Batch size 64, 30 epochs.}

  \smallsep

  \subheading{Evaluation Metrics}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  Precision, Recall, F1-score, Intersection-over-Union (IoU),
  and end-to-end detection latency measured from image
  acquisition to alert generation.}

  \subheading{Per-Region Generalization}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  WildFire-Net maintains strong performance across all seven
  climate zones (F1 range: 0.892--0.948), compared to
  ResNet-50 (F1 range: 0.731--0.864). The attention modules
  are particularly effective in Mediterranean and boreal
  regions where smoke dispersion patterns differ from
  temperate training data.}

  \smallsep

  \begin{resultbox}
    {\footnotesize\bfseries\color{secondary}Key Finding:}
    {\footnotesize\color{darktext} The spatial-channel
    attention mechanism improves cross-region F1 by
    8.3 percentage points compared to the same architecture
    without attention, confirming that attention helps the
    model focus on fire-specific spectral signatures rather
    than region-specific background features.}
  \end{resultbox}

  \subheading{Ablation Study}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  Removing spatial attention reduces F1 by 5.2pp; removing
  channel attention reduces F1 by 4.1pp; removing both
  reduces F1 by 9.7pp. Two-stage transfer learning contributes
  6.8pp over single-stage fine-tuning, validating the
  importance of intermediate domain adaptation.}

  \paneltitle{\faLightbulb\hspace{4pt}Conclusions \& Resources}

  \smallsep

  \subheading{Summary}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  This study demonstrates that deep transfer learning with
  attention mechanisms can achieve state-of-the-art wildfire
  detection from satellite imagery while maintaining
  sufficient efficiency for edge deployment. WildFire-Net
  outperforms existing operational systems by 12--18\% and
  generalizes robustly across diverse ecosystems.}

  \smallsep

  \subheading{Limitations}

  {\footnotesize\color{darktext}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Detection of small fires ($<$ 0.5 ha) remains
      challenging due to spatial resolution constraints
      (10--30 m per pixel).
    \item Nighttime detection relies exclusively on thermal
      bands, reducing accuracy to $\sim$82\%.
    \item Cloud cover obscures optical imagery; integration
      with SAR data is needed for all-weather capability.
  \end{itemize}
  }

  \smallsep

  \subheading{Future Work}

  \begin{accentstrip}
    {\footnotesize\color{darktext}\textbf{Drone Integration:}
    Extending the framework to ingest high-resolution UAV
    imagery for verification of satellite-based detections.}
  \end{accentstrip}

  \begin{accentstrip}
    {\footnotesize\color{darktext}\textbf{Multi-Modal Fusion:}
    Combining optical, thermal, and synthetic aperture radar
    (SAR) data for robust all-weather, day-night detection.}
  \end{accentstrip}

  \begin{accentstrip}

\sectionheader{Narrative Observation Notes}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=white,colframe=borderlight,boxrule=0.5pt,arc=3pt,left=14pt,right=14pt,top=12pt,bottom=12pt]
\textcolor{primary}{\textbf{Lesson Opening (10:15--10:22 AM)}}

Ms.\ Chen began with a warm-up problem displayed on the board: \textquotedblleft{}Graph \textit{y = 2x + 1} and identify the slope and y-intercept.\textquotedblright{} Students completed this individually in their notebooks while she took attendance and checked homework completion. After three minutes, she projected a student's correctly graphed solution using the document camera and briefly reviewed slope-intercept form. The transition to the day's objective was smooth, anchored by a relatable scenario: \textquotedblleft{}Two ticket vendors are selling concert tickets with different pricing structures --- how do we figure out which one is the better deal?\textquotedblright{}

\vspace{8pt}
\textcolor{primary}{\textbf{Direct Instruction \& Guided Practice (10:22--10:40 AM)}}

Using the \textquotedblleft{}I Do\textquotedblright{} phase, Ms.\ Chen modeled solving the system \textit{y = 50x + 20} and \textit{y = 35x + 50} by graphing both lines on the coordinate plane, narrating each step clearly. During the \textquotedblleft{}We Do\textquotedblright{} phase, students guided her through a second example while she recorded their input on the board. The class then moved into paired practice with six progressively challenging systems. Ms.\ Chen circulated purposefully, spending extra time with pairs that had made scaling errors on their axes --- a common misconception she addressed by asking, \textquotedblleft{}What does each square on your grid represent?\textquotedblright{}

\vspace{8pt}
\textcolor{primary}{\textbf{Closure \& Debrief (10:40--10:50 AM)}}

The planned exit ticket was not distributed. Instead, Ms.\ Chen called on three students to summarize the key concept and asked the whole class a closing reflection question. While the verbal summary was accurate, several students were visibly packing up during the final two minutes, suggesting the closure lost some instructional impact.
\end{tcolorbox}

\sectionheader{Strengths}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=ratingshade!40,colframe=accent,boxrule=0.8pt,arc=3pt,left=14pt,right=14pt,top=10pt,bottom=10pt]
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=5pt,topsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Positive Classroom Climate:}\ \ Ms.\ Chen has cultivated a learning environment where students feel safe, respected, and motivated to participate. Her practice of greeting students by name and using affirming language sets a standard worth highlighting.
  \item \textbf{Deep Content Knowledge:}\ \ Her ability to connect systems of equations to real-world contexts and prior learning about linear functions reflects strong mathematical understanding and thoughtful lesson design.
  \item \textbf{Effective Collaborative Structures:}\ \ The use of Think-Pair-Share and paired graphing activities kept students actively engaged. The \textquotedblleft{}I Do, We Do, You Do\textquotedblright{} framework provided a clear and supportive progression.
  \item \textbf{Purposeful Circulation and Feedback:}\ \ While moving through the room, Ms.\ Chen delivered feedback that was specific, actionable, and focused on student thinking rather than just answer correctness.
\end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\vspace{10pt}

\sectionheader{Areas for Growth}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=highlight!15,colframe=highlight,boxrule=0.8pt,arc=3pt,left=14pt,right=14pt,top=10pt,bottom=10pt]
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=5pt,topsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Questioning for Higher-Order Thinking:}\ \ The majority of questions posed during the lesson were at the recall and comprehension level. Incorporating more analysis and evaluation prompts --- such as comparing solution methods or predicting outcomes --- would deepen student reasoning.
  \item \textbf{Differentiation for Diverse Learners:}\ \ While the core activity was strong, additional scaffolds for English Language Learners and struggling students were limited. Consider providing sentence frames, bilingual glossaries, or targeted small-group instruction during independent practice.
  \item \textbf{Lesson Closure and Formative Assessment Routines:}\ \ The exit ticket was planned but not executed, and the closure felt abbreviated. Establishing a protected five-minute wrap-up routine would ensure consistent formative data collection and reinforce key takeaways.
  \item \textbf{Monitoring Participation Equity:}\ \ A small group of students near the back of the room did not contribute during whole-class discussion. Implementing randomized response strategies or structured turn-taking protocols could broaden participation.
\end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\vspace{12pt}

\sectionheader{Summary \& Recommendations}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=lightbg,colframe=borderlight,boxrule=0.5pt,arc=3pt,left=14pt,right=14pt,top=12pt,bottom=12pt]
\textbf{Overall Observation Outcome:}\ \ Proficient. Ms.\ Chen demonstrates solid instructional skills, strong relationships with students, and a commitment to continuous professional growth. Her lesson was thoughtfully planned, well-executed through the majority of the period, and reflected a student-centered approach to mathematics instruction.

\vspace{6pt}
\textbf{Priority Action Steps:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt,topsep=4pt]
  \item Plan and implement at least two higher-order thinking questions per lesson, using a question stem bank aligned to Bloom's Taxonomy as a planning tool.
  \item Develop a differentiated support toolkit for Algebra I that includes visual organizers, sentence starters, and tiered practice problems for ELL students and struggling learners.
  \item Implement a consistent closure routine (final 5--7 minutes of every class) that includes an exit ticket, a brief written reflection, or a structured pair-share summary.
  \item Use an intentional participation tracking method (e.g., a seating-chart checklist or equity sticks) to ensure all students have opportunities to contribute during whole-class discussion.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
\thispagestyle{empty}

Modern cloud platforms host thousands of tenants whose workloads compete for
shared compute, memory, and network resources. Static or rule-based
schedulers struggle to adapt to the volatility of multi-tenant demand,
frequently leading to resource contention, degraded latency, and violated
service-level agreements (SLAs). This thesis proposes an adaptive scheduling
framework that formulates resource allocation as a sequential
decision-making problem and solves it using deep reinforcement learning
(RL). A scheduler agent observes cluster telemetry and per-tenant queue
state, and learns a policy that balances utilization, latency, and SLA
compliance through a composite reward signal. The proposed method is
evaluated against round-robin and static-threshold baselines on a simulated
multi-tenant cluster workload. Results show a 23\% reduction in average
latency and a 41\% reduction in SLA violations relative to the strongest
baseline, at a modest increase in scheduling overhead. These findings
suggest that learning-based schedulers are a practical path toward
self-adaptive resource management in multi-tenant cloud environments.

\vspace{10pt}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} cloud computing, resource scheduling,
multi-tenancy, reinforcement learning, quality of service.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}
Public and private cloud platforms increasingly serve a multi-tenant model,
where independent workloads from different customers share the same
physical infrastructure. Efficient scheduling of compute, memory, and
network bandwidth across tenants is central to both provider profitability
and customer-facing performance guarantees. Traditional schedulers rely on
static heuristics---round-robin, proportional-share, or fixed
thresholds---that were designed for relatively predictable workloads. As
tenant demand becomes bursty and heterogeneous, these heuristics leave
performance on the table: resources sit idle while other tenants queue, or
are over-committed until an SLA is breached.

\section{Problem Statement}
Given a shared cluster of $N$ compute nodes serving $M$ tenants with
time-varying workloads, the scheduling problem is to assign incoming
requests to nodes such that (i) overall resource utilization is maximized,
(ii) per-tenant latency stays within contracted bounds, and (iii) SLA
violations are minimized, all under incomplete knowledge of future demand.
This is a sequential decision problem under uncertainty, which motivates a
learning-based rather than a purely rule-based solution.

\section{Research Objectives}
This thesis pursues the following objectives:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{O\arabic*.}, leftmargin=2.2em]
  \item Formulate multi-tenant cloud scheduling as a Markov decision process
        suitable for reinforcement learning.
  \item Design a composite reward function that jointly captures
        utilization, latency, and SLA compliance.
  \item Implement and train a scheduling agent, and benchmark it against
        established baseline policies.
  \item Analyze the trade-off between scheduling quality and computational
        overhead introduced by the learned policy.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Contributions}
The main contributions of this work are: a reward formulation that unifies
three competing scheduling objectives into a single tunable signal; a
lightweight state representation that scales to clusters with hundreds of
nodes; and an empirical evaluation showing consistent gains over
non-adaptive baselines across three synthetic workload traces.

\section{Thesis Organization}
Chapter~2 reviews related work in cloud scheduling and learning-based
systems control. Chapter~3 presents the proposed methodology, including
system architecture and reward design. Chapter~4 reports experimental
results. Chapter~5 concludes and outlines future work.

\section{Reinforcement Learning for Systems Control}
Reinforcement learning has been applied to a range of systems problems,
from database index tuning to network congestion control, where the
underlying decision process is sequential and the effect of an action is
only observable after a delay. Applying RL to scheduling requires careful
state and reward design so that the agent optimizes for outcomes that
matter operationally, rather than proxy metrics that are easy to compute
but weakly correlated with tenant-perceived quality.

\chapter{Proposed Methodology}

\section{System Architecture}
Figure~\ref{fig:arch} shows the overall pipeline. Incoming tenant requests
are summarized by a feature extractor into a fixed-length state vector,
which is consumed by the RL scheduler agent. The agent's action is a
placement decision handed to the cluster resource manager, which enacts it
on the compute nodes. Utilization telemetry is fed back to the agent to
close the control loop.

\section{Reward Function}
The agent optimizes a composite reward that balances three competing
objectives: utilization $U_t$, normalized latency $L_t$, and an SLA
violation indicator $V_t$, observed at each scheduling step $t$:
\begin{equation}
R_t = \alpha \, U_t \;-\; \beta \, L_t \;-\; \gamma \, V_t,
\qquad \alpha + \beta + \gamma = 1,
\label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $\gamma$ are tunable weights. The agent is
trained to maximize the discounted return
\begin{equation}
J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}
\left[ \sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma_d^{\,t} R_t \right],
\label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
where $\pi_\theta$ is the policy parameterized by $\theta$ and $\gamma_d \in
(0,1)$ is the discount factor.

\section{Training Procedure}
The agent is trained with a policy-gradient method using trajectories
collected from a cluster simulator seeded with three synthetic workload
traces (steady, bursty, and diurnal). Training proceeds in episodes of
fixed length, with the reward weights in Equation~\ref{eq:reward} annealed
from utilization-favoring to SLA-favoring over the first third of training
to encourage early exploration.

\section{Discussion}
The proposed agent reduces average latency by 23\% and SLA violations by
41\% relative to the strongest baseline (control-theoretic), while also
improving mean utilization by eight percentage points. The gain is largest
under bursty and diurnal traces, where static policies cannot react quickly
enough to shifting demand. The main cost is a modest increase in
per-decision compute overhead, discussed further in Section~4.2 of the full
thesis manuscript.

\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}

This thesis presented a reinforcement-learning-based scheduler for
multi-tenant cloud clusters, built around a composite reward that jointly
targets utilization, latency, and SLA compliance. Experiments on simulated
workloads show consistent improvements over round-robin, static-threshold,
and control-theoretic baselines. Future work includes evaluating the
approach on live cluster traces, extending the state representation to
capture cross-tenant fairness constraints, and studying the stability of
the learned policy under adversarial tenant behavior.

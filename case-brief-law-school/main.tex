% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Facts}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Oliver L.\ Brown, a Black resident of Topeka, Kansas, attempted to enroll his daughter
Linda Brown in Sumner Elementary School, a white-only public school seven blocks from their
home.  The Board of Education denied admission pursuant to a Kansas statute permitting
(but not requiring) cities of more than 15{,}000 inhabitants to maintain separate school
facilities for Black and white children.  Linda was instead required to travel approximately
21 blocks to Monroe Elementary School, the nearest Black school.

Four consolidated cases from Kansas, South Carolina, Virginia, and Delaware challenged
segregated public school systems maintained under state law.  In each jurisdiction,
Black children had been denied admission to schools attended by white children under
laws requiring or permitting segregation.  While the facilities in some locales were
being equalized in physical terms, segregation as a policy remained.

The cases were initially heard by three-judge federal district courts; each court applied
the ``separate but equal'' doctrine established in \textit{Plessy v.\ Ferguson},
163 U.S.\ 537 (1896), and denied relief.  Only the Delaware court ordered admission to
white schools because the Black schools were found to be inferior.  All parties then
petitioned the Supreme Court.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Issue}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{tcolorbox}[issuebox]
  Does the racial segregation of children in public schools solely on the basis of race,
  even when the physical facilities and other \legalterm{tangible} factors may be equal,
  deprive the minority children of equal educational opportunities, and thereby violate the
  Equal Protection Clause of the \textbf{Fourteenth Amendment} to the United States
  Constitution?
\end{tcolorbox}

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Holding}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{tcolorbox}[holdingbox]
  \textbf{Yes.}  Separate educational facilities are \textbf{inherently unequal}.
  Segregation in public schools on the basis of race violates the Equal Protection Clause
  of the Fourteenth Amendment, regardless of whether the physical facilities and tangible
  factors are equal.  The Court unanimously overruled \textit{Plessy v.\ Ferguson}
  insofar as it applied to public education.
\end{tcolorbox}

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Reasoning}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Historical Ambiguity of the Fourteenth Amendment}

Chief Justice Warren acknowledged that the historical record surrounding the adoption of
the Fourteenth Amendment was ``inconclusive'' with respect to its intended application to
segregated public schools.  Public education in 1868 was rudimentary in the South; the
question of segregation in schools had not been the subject of substantial congressional
debate.  The Court therefore declined to resolve the case on original intent grounds and
instead examined the \textit{current} role of public education.

\subsection{Importance of Education in Modern Society}

\setcounter{reasonstep}{0}
\reasonstep{Public education as the foundation of citizenship.}
The Court emphasized that education is ``perhaps the most important function of state and
local governments.''  Compulsory school attendance laws and substantial public expenditure
testify to its importance in a democratic society.  A child denied a good education is
unlikely to succeed.

\reasonstep{``Separate but equal'' cannot be squared with modern social science.}
Drawing on empirical social-science evidence, including the \textit{doll studies} of
Dr.\ Kenneth Clark, the Court found that segregation generated a ``feeling of inferiority''
in Black children as to their status in the community, affecting their motivation to learn.
This psychological harm was compounded where state law sanctioned it.

\reasonstep{Intangible factors render separate schools unequal.}
Even where physical facilities were being equalized, the intangible components of
educational opportunity --- the ability to study, engage, and interact with students of other
races --- remained unequal.  The Court cited its own precedents in \textit{Sweatt v.\ Painter}
and \textit{McLaurin v.\ Oklahoma} to establish that intangible factors matter.

\reasonstep{The Equal Protection Clause forbids such inequality.}
Whatever may have been the extent of psychological knowledge at the time of \textit{Plessy},
the 1954 Court held that this finding of inferiority could not be erased by physical
equalization.  Separate educational facilities are, by their very nature, unequal.

\subsection{Scope of the Decision}

The Court limited its holding to public education and expressly reserved the question of
remedy, scheduling further argument --- resulting in \textit{Brown II} (1955), which ordered
desegregation ``with all deliberate speed.''

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Concurrences \& Dissents}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{rvcomment}{Concurrence --- Chief Justice Warren (for a unanimous Court)}
  The decision was deliberately rendered unanimous, at Chief Justice Warren's urging,
  to present a united judicial front on so momentous a constitutional question.  There
  were no separate concurrences or dissents.  Justices who had privately expressed
  reservations --- including Justice Robert H.\ Jackson and Justice Stanley F.\ Reed ---
  ultimately joined the opinion.  Justice Jackson, hospitalized with a heart attack,
  left the hospital to be present for the announcement.
\end{rvcomment}

\begin{rvcomment}{Historical Contrast --- \textit{Plessy v.\ Ferguson} (1896), Harlan, J., dissenting}
  Justice John Marshall Harlan's celebrated dissent in \textit{Plessy} anticipated
  \textit{Brown} by nearly 60 years: ``Our Constitution is color-blind, and neither
  knows nor tolerates classes among citizens.''  Harlan alone argued that the Louisiana
  ``Separate Car Act'' violated the Thirteenth and Fourteenth Amendments.
  \textit{Brown} vindicated Harlan's dissent.
\end{rvcomment}

\section{Significance \& Doctrinal Impact}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}>{\color{accent}\bfseries}l@{\hspace{8mm}}>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}X@{}}
  Constitutional  & Definitively established that the Equal Protection Clause of the
                    Fourteenth Amendment prohibits racial segregation in public schools;
                    repudiated \textit{Plessy v.\ Ferguson} in the education context.         \\[3pt]
  Civil Rights    & Catalyzed the modern civil rights movement; provided the constitutional
                    foundation for the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights
                    Act of 1965.                                                               \\[3pt]
  Judicial Role   & Demonstrated the Court's willingness to rely on social science evidence
                    in constitutional adjudication, triggering debate over \legalterm{judicial
                    empiricism}.                                                               \\[3pt]
  Education Law   & Extended through subsequent cases to prohibit segregation in higher
                    education (\textit{Green v.\ County School Bd.}, 391 U.S.\ 430 (1968);
                    \textit{Swann v.\ Charlotte-Mecklenburg}, 402 U.S.\ 1 (1971)).            \\[3pt]
  Precedent       & Repeatedly cited as authority for the proposition that
                    \textit{separate is inherently unequal} beyond the education context.     \\
\end{tabularx}

\section{Key Quotes}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{tcolorbox}[sidenotebox]
  \textbf{Chief Justice Warren (majority):}\\[3pt]
  ``We conclude that, in the field of public education, the doctrine of `separate but equal'
  has no place.  Separate educational facilities are \textbf{inherently unequal}.  Therefore,
  we hold that the plaintiffs and others similarly situated for whom the actions have been
  brought are, by reason of the segregation complained of, deprived of the equal protection
  of the laws guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment.''
  \hfill\textit{347 U.S.\ at 495}
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[sidenotebox]
  \textbf{Chief Justice Warren (on psychology of segregation):}\\[3pt]
  ``To separate [Black children] from others of similar age and qualifications solely
  because of their race generates a feeling of inferiority as to their status in the community
  that may affect their hearts and minds in a way unlikely ever to be undone.''
  \hfill\textit{347 U.S.\ at 494}
\end{tcolorbox}

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Exam / Bar Notes}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{tcolorbox}[sidenotebox]
  \textbf{\color{navyblue}Analytical Checklist for Equal Protection (post-\textit{Brown})}\\[4pt]
  \begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}>{\color{navyblue}\bfseries}l@{\hspace{6mm}}>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}X@{}}
    Step 1 & Identify the classification (race $\Rightarrow$ strict scrutiny).               \\[2pt]
    Step 2 & Determine whether a fundamental right or suspect class is implicated.           \\[2pt]
    Step 3 & Apply appropriate tier: strict / intermediate / rational basis.                  \\[2pt]
    Step 4 & Assess government interest (compelling vs.\ substantial vs.\ legitimate).       \\[2pt]
    Step 5 & Assess means (narrowly tailored vs.\ substantially related vs.\ rational).      \\[2pt]
    Step 6 & Under strict scrutiny, segregation by race in public schools is per se invalid. \\
  \end{tabularx}
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em, itemsep=2pt, topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Standard:} Strict scrutiny applies to racial classifications; the government bears the burden.
  \item \textbf{Overruling:} \textit{Plessy}'s ``separate but equal'' doctrine survives only outside the \textit{Brown} context --- note continuing debates in higher education (\textit{Grutter}, \textit{Fisher}, \textit{SFFA v.\ Harvard} (2023)).
  \item \textbf{Distinguish:} \textit{Brown} invalidates \textit{de jure} segregation (state-mandated); \textit{de facto} segregation (residential patterns) is analyzed differently.
  \item \textbf{Mooting risk:} A challenge to a voluntary desegregation plan may moot the claim if the school board subsequently discontinues it (\textit{Parents Involved}).
\end{itemize}

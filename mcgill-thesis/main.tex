\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

\begin{abstractbox}
\noindent This thesis investigates the rheology and segregation dynamics of dense granular flows through confined geometries, with emphasis on bidisperse mixtures and non-local constitutive modelling. Using a combination of discrete element method (DEM) simulations and laboratory experiments in an annular shear cell, we characterize velocity profiles, packing fraction distributions, and particle-size segregation patterns under a range of confining pressures and shear rates spanning three orders of magnitude.

\vspace{8pt}

\noindent
\textit{Cette th\`ese porte sur la rh\'eologie et la dynamique de s\'egr\'egation des \'ecoulements granulaires denses en g\'eom\'etries confin\'ees, en mettant l'accent sur les m\'elanges bidisperses et la mod\'elisation constitutive non locale. \`A l'aide de simulations par m\'ethode des \'el\'ements discrets (DEM) et d'exp\'eriences en laboratoire dans une cellule de cisaillement annulaire, nous caract\'erisons les profils de vitesse, les distributions de fraction volumique et les motifs de s\'egr\'egation par taille de particules sous une gamme de pressions de confinement et de taux de cisaillement couvrant trois ordres de grandeur.}
\end{abstractbox}

Implications for geotechnical applications and industrial granular-material handling processes are also discussed.}

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Motivation and Context}

Granular materials --- collections of discrete, macroscopic particles that interact primarily through contact forces --- are ubiquitous in nature and industry. From pharmaceutical powder blending to geophysical debris flows, from agricultural grain handling to planetary regolith dynamics, the ability to predict and control granular flow behaviour carries enormous practical significance. Despite centuries of study, a unified continuum description of granular rheology remains elusive, particularly in the dense, slow-flowing regime where non-local effects dominate \cite{miidi2004,daCruz2005,Jop2006}.

This thesis addresses two interconnected challenges in granular mechanics. First, we seek to characterize how geometric confinement alters the constitutive response of dense granular flows, extending beyond the well-studied planar shear configuration to annular and converging geometries relevant to industrial silo discharge and geophysical channel flows. Second, we develop and validate a non-local extension of the $\mu(I)$ rheology that accounts for cooperative particle rearrangements near the jamming transition.

\section{Thesis Outline}

The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows. \textbf{Chapter~2} reviews the fundamental framework of granular rheology, from the inertial number $I$ to the $\mu(I)$ constitutive law, and surveys experimental evidence for non-local effects in confined flows. \textbf{Chapter~3} describes the discrete element method (DEM) simulation methodology, the annular shear cell experimental apparatus, and the data analysis pipeline. \textbf{Chapter~4} presents our results on bidisperse segregation and velocity profile characterization under steady dense flow conditions. \textbf{Chapter~5} introduces our non-local rheological model, derives the cooperativity length scale from DEM microstructural data, and validates the model against independent experiments. \textbf{Chapter~6} summarizes the contributions, discusses limitations, and outlines directions for future research.

\chapter{Background and Literature Review}

\section{The $\mu(I)$ Rheology}

\subsection{Dimensional Analysis of Dry Granular Flows}

Consider a collection of stiff, frictional grains of mean diameter $d$ and material density $\rho_p$, subjected to a confining pressure $P$ and sheared at a rate $\dot{\gamma}$. Dimensional analysis \cite{miidi2004,daCruz2005} identifies a single dimensionless parameter governing the flow regime:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:inertial}
I = \frac{\dot{\gamma} d}{\sqrt{P / \rho_p}},
\end{equation}

termed the \textit{inertial number}. This parameter may be interpreted as the ratio of the microscopic inertial timescale $d/\sqrt{P/\rho_p}$ (the time for a particle to rearrange under the imposed pressure) to the macroscopic deformation timescale $1/\dot{\gamma}$. The quasi-static regime corresponds to $I \to 0$, while rapid collisional flows occupy $I \gtrsim 0.3$.

\subsection{Constitutive Relations}

In the dense regime ($10^{-3} \lesssim I \lesssim 10^{-1}$), the effective friction coefficient $\mu \equiv \tau / P$ and the solid volume fraction $\phi$ are well described by the empirical forms \cite{Jop2006}:

\begin{align}
\mu(I) &= \mu_s + \frac{\mu_2 - \mu_s}{I_0/I + 1}, \label{eq:muI}\\
\phi(I) &= \phi_{\max} - (\phi_{\max} - \phi_{\min})\, I. \label{eq:phiI}
\end{align}

Here $\mu_s$ is the static friction coefficient (yield threshold), $\mu_2$ is the limiting friction at large $I$, $I_0$ is a material-dependent reference inertial number, and $\phi_{\max}$, $\phi_{\min}$ are the random-close-packing and critical-state packing fractions, respectively. Typical parameter values for glass beads are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:parameters}.

\section{Non-Local Effects Near Jamming}

For flows approaching the yield threshold ($I \lesssim 10^{-3}$), the local $\mu(I)$ description breaks down: the stress at a point depends not only on the local strain rate but also on the deformation field in a surrounding \textit{cooperativity} region of size $\xi$. This phenomenon, first identified in emulsions and soft glasses \cite{Goyon2008,Kamrin2012}, has been extended to dry granular media through the non-local granular fluidity model.

\begin{theorembox}[title=Non-local granular fluidity model]
The granular fluidity $g \equiv \dot{\gamma}/\mu$ satisfies the inhomogeneous Helmholtz equation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fluidity}
\xi^2 \nabla^2 g = g - g_{\text{loc}}(\mu, P),
\end{equation}
where $g_{\text{loc}}$ is the local fluidity predicted by inverting Eqs.~(\ref{eq:muI})--(\ref{eq:phiI}), and $\xi$ is the cooperativity length, which scales as $\xi \propto d\,(|\mu - \mu_s|/\mu_s)^{-1/2}$ near the yield threshold.
\end{theorembox}

Equation~(\ref{eq:fluidity}) introduces a mesoscopic length scale $\xi$ that smooths the fluidity field over regions where cooperative particle rearrangements dominate the mechanical response. Determining $\xi$ from microstructural data is a central objective of the experimental programme in Chapter~4.

\chapter{Methodology}

\section{Discrete Element Method Simulations}

The three-dimensional DEM simulations employ a linear spring-dashpot contact model with Coulomb friction, implemented in the open-source code \texttt{GranuLo}\footnote{\texttt{GranuLo} is a fictional DEM solver developed at the Granular Media Laboratory, McGill University.} optimized for shared-memory parallel architectures. Particle–particle and particle–wall contacts follow:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{ij} = \bigl(k_n \delta_n - \gamma_n \dot{\delta}_n\bigr)\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij}
                  + \min\bigl(\|k_t \boldsymbol{\delta}_t + \gamma_t \dot{\boldsymbol{\delta}}_t\|,\;
                            \mu_p \|\mathbf{F}_n\|\bigr)\,\hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij},
\end{equation}

where $k_n$, $k_t$ are normal and tangential stiffnesses, $\gamma_n$, $\gamma_t$ are viscous damping coefficients, $\mu_p$ is the inter-particle friction coefficient, $\boldsymbol{\delta}_{n,t}$ are the normal and tangential overlap vectors, and $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij}$, $\hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij}$ are unit normal and tangent vectors at the contact.

\subsection*{Tangential Velocity Decay}

The azimuthally averaged tangential velocity $\langle v_\theta\rangle(r)$ exhibits a characteristic exponential decay from the inner wall:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:velocity_decay}
\langle v_\theta\rangle(r) = \Omega R_{\text{in}}\;
\exp\!\left(-\frac{r - R_{\text{in}}}{\lambda}\right),
\end{equation}

where $\lambda$ is the shear-band width. Table~\ref{tab:shear_band} reports $\lambda$ extracted from fits to Eq.~(\ref{eq:velocity_decay}) across three confining pressures and two size ratios.

\section{Non-Local Model Validation}

We fit the non-local fluidity model (Eq.~\ref{eq:fluidity}) to the experimental velocity profiles, treating the cooperativity length $\xi$ as the sole adjustable parameter. Figure~\ref{fig:fluidity_fit} (not shown) demonstrates quantitative agreement between the non-local prediction and DEM data across the full range of $I$, whereas the local $\mu(I)$ model systematically underestimates velocity in the quasi-static tail of the shear band. The extracted cooperativity length follows $\xi / d \approx 3.2 \pm 0.4$ near the yield threshold, in reasonable agreement with the scaling $\xi/d \propto (|\mu - \mu_s|/\mu_s)^{-0.49 \pm 0.07}$ derived from independent microstructural correlation analysis.

\chapter{Conclusion}

This thesis has advanced the understanding of dense granular flows in confined geometries through a combined experimental--numerical programme. The principal contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\textbullet]
  \item \textbf{Comprehensive characterization} of velocity and packing-fraction profiles in an annular shear cell over three decades of shear rate and three confining pressures, establishing benchmark datasets for bidisperse granular rheology.
  \item \textbf{Quantification of size-segregation dynamics}, demonstrating that the percolation velocity of small particles scales as $v_{\text{seg}} \propto \dot{\gamma} d_S (d_L/d_S - 1)$ in the dense regime, with a pre-factor that depends weakly on $I$.
  \item \textbf{Formulation and validation of a non-local $\mu(I)$ rheology} incorporating a cooperativity length $\xi$ that captures the spatial extent of plastic events near jamming, bridging the gap between quasi-static and inertial flow descriptions.
  \item \textbf{Open-source release} of the \texttt{GranuLo} DEM codebase and the experimental dataset, enabling reproducible research and future model development.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Future Work}

Several directions merit further investigation. The cooperativity length $\xi$ has been determined only for spherical particles; extending the analysis to angular grains and cohesive powders would test the universality of the non-local framework. Three-dimensional imaging via X-ray microtomography could provide direct measurements of the microstructural correlation functions that underlie Eq.~(\ref{eq:fluidity}). Finally, coupling the validated rheology with continuum-scale CFD codes would enable predictive simulations of industrial-scale granular handling equipment at Vertex Process Technologies and similar facilities.

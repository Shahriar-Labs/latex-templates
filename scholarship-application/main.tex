\section{Personal Statement}

{\color{accent}\bfseries Research Focus:\ }
The Political Economy of Climate Adaptation in Small Island Developing States.

My doctoral research examines how institutional capacity and international funding
mechanisms shape climate resilience outcomes across the Caribbean and Pacific SIDS.
Drawing on mixed-methods fieldwork in Barbados, Fiji, and the Maldives, I have developed
a novel framework linking governance quality indices to measurable adaptation success
rates. This work addresses a critical gap: quantitative political-economy approaches
remain strikingly underrepresented in the SIDS literature.

During my M.Phil.\ at Oxford (2020--2022), I co-authored a policy brief adopted by the
Commonwealth Secretariat's climate finance division. At the World Bank (2022--2024), I
contributed to the \$450M Pacific Resilience Program, designing monitoring and evaluation
protocols now deployed across 12 countries. These experiences sharpened my conviction
that rigorous academic research must directly inform multilateral policy design.

\section{Proposed Research Plan}

The Rhodes Scholarship would enable me to pursue the D.Phil.\ in Public Policy at the
Blavatnik School of Government, Oxford. My proposed dissertation extends my M.Phil.\
findings through three methodological innovations:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt, nosep, itemsep=2pt, before=\vspace{0.2em}]
  \item A panel dataset covering 38 SIDS over 20 years (2005--2025), merging IMF fiscal
        data with satellite-derived climate vulnerability indices.
  \item A quasi-experimental design exploiting exogenous variation in Green Climate Fund
        disbursement timing to estimate causal effects on institutional reform capacity.
  \item Qualitative process-tracing in Barbados, Fiji, Maldives, and Samoa to validate
        causal mechanisms identified in the quantitative analysis.
\end{itemize}

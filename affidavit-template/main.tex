% SECTION I: QUALIFICATIONS

  \textbf{\textcolor{LegalNavy}{\MakeUppercase{I. Background and Qualifications}}}
\end{center}
\par\vspace{0.3em}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*., leftmargin=0.4in, rightmargin=0.1in, align=left]
  \item I am over the age of eighteen (18) and am competent to make this Sworn Affidavit. The facts set forth herein are based upon my personal knowledge, my forensic examination of the digital evidence, and my professional analysis of the systems in question.
  
  \item I hold a Doctor of Philosophy (Ph.D.) in Computer Science from the Apex Technical Institute, specializing in distributed systems and database transaction consistency. I have over fifteen (15) years of professional experience in database forensics and cloud architecture.
  
  \item I currently serve as the Principal Investigator for Rostova Forensic Consulting, LLC. Prior to this role, I was a Senior Database Architect at Meridian Systems and led the Distributed Systems Engineering team at Nimbus Core. I have been certified as a Forensic Systems Analyst (CFSA) since 2015.
  
  \item I have previously testified as an expert witness in state and federal courts, as well as in binding commercial arbitrations, on nine (9) occasions concerning database failures, data migration breaches, and intellectual property disputes.
\end{enumerate}

\par\vspace{1.0em}

% SECTION II: SCOPE OF ENGAGEMENT
\begin{center}
  \textbf{\textcolor{LegalNavy}{\MakeUppercase{II. Scope of Investigation and Materials Reviewed}}}
\end{center}
\par\vspace{0.3em}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*., leftmargin=0.4in, rightmargin=0.1in, align=left]
  \setcounter{enumi}{4}
  \item I was retained by legal counsel for the Claimant, Synapse Technologies, Inc. (``Synapse''), to conduct an independent forensic audit of the data migration incident that occurred between April 12, 2026, and April 14, 2026. The objective of this investigation was to determine the cause of the data loss that occurred during the decommissioning and migration of the database servers hosted by the Respondent, Vertex Global Solutions, LLC (``Vertex'').
  
  \item In conducting my analysis, I reviewed the following materials, files, and database images:
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=0.3in]
    \item Full server transaction logs from the Vertex database node (\texttt{vdb-node-04}) spanning April 10, 2026, through April 15, 2026;
    \item Configuration files for the Meridian Migration Engine (version 4.2.1-prod);
    \item Database schema definitions for the Synapse application server;
    \item The Service Level Agreement (SLA) and Migration Protocol Agreement signed by Synapse and Vertex on January 15, 2026;
    \item System logs, error reports, and database dump files extracted from the target Synapse destination cluster.
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\par\vspace{1.0em}

% SECTION III: CHRONOLOGY OF THE INCIDENT
\begin{center}
  \textbf{\textcolor{LegalNavy}{\MakeUppercase{III. Chronology of the Migration Incident}}}
\end{center}
\par\vspace{0.3em}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*., leftmargin=0.4in, rightmargin=0.1in, align=left]
  \setcounter{enumi}{6}
  \item On April 12, 2026, at approximately 22:00:00 Mountain Standard Time (MST), the scheduled database migration began. The task was designed to move approximately 4.8 terabytes of active customer data from the Vertex-hosted legacy SQL cluster to Synapse's newly established cloud instance (the ``Nimbus Database'').
  
  \item According to the system logs, the Meridian Migration Engine successfully initialized and executed Phases 1 through 3, which involved copying static configuration tables and archived read-only data.
  
  \item At 22:14:32 MST, during Phase 4 (live transactional synchronization), the migration logs report a critical synchronization lock on the primary ledger table, \texttt{nexa\_ledger}. This lock halted the forward-replication queue, creating a backlog of unprocessed transactions in the cache.
  
  \item At 22:15:10 MST, the logs indicate that a force-override command (command ID: \texttt{SYS\_FORCE\_BYPASS}) was executed from the IP address 192.168.42.105, which is associated with a Vertex network administrator's internal workstation.
  
  \item This bypass command forced the migration engine to skip the verification of standard transaction boundaries, effectively terminating the database locks and forcing the system to write data to the target Nimbus Database without verifying foreign key integrity or transactional consistency.
\end{enumerate}

\par\vspace{1.0em}

% SECTION IV: TECHNICAL FAILURE ANALYSIS
\begin{center}
  \textbf{\textcolor{LegalNavy}{\MakeUppercase{IV. Technical Analysis of the Apex Core Failure}}}
\end{center}
\par\vspace{0.3em}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*., leftmargin=0.4in, rightmargin=0.1in, align=left]
  \setcounter{enumi}{11}
  \item My technical analysis focuses on the bypass of the standard transactional safety protocols, specifically the \textbf{Apex Core} synchronization framework. The Apex Core protocol is designed to ensure strict ACID (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability) compliance across distributed nodes.
  
  \item When the bypass command was executed, it deactivated the two-phase commit (2PC) mechanism of the Meridian Migration Engine. Under normal operations, a two-phase commit ensures that a transaction is either committed on both systems (Vertex and Synapse) or rolled back entirely if a failure occurs.
  
  \item By deactivating the two-phase commit mechanism, the database entered an inconsistent state. Transactions were marked as ``completed'' on the source Vertex servers but were never successfully written to the destination Nimbus Database.
  
  \item Specifically, a total of 14,242 transaction blocks (amounting to approximately 14.2 gigabytes of critical financial and user data) were dropped. The relevant error logs are summarized in the table below:
  
  \par\vspace{0.8em}
  \noindent
  \begin{minipage}{\textwidth}
    \centering
    \small
    \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{l X l r}
      \toprule
      \textbf{Timestamp} & \textbf{Affected Table} & \textbf{Error Code} & \textbf{Dropped} \\
      \midrule
      22:14:35 & \texttt{nexa\_ledger} & \texttt{ERR\_LOCK\_TIMEOUT} & 4,812 \\
      22:15:12 & \texttt{synapse\_users} & \texttt{ERR\_FK\_VIOLATION} & 2,904 \\
      22:15:45 & \texttt{meridian\_billing} & \texttt{ERR\_WRITE\_ABORT} & 5,110 \\
      22:16:10 & \texttt{apex\_sessions} & \texttt{ERR\_DIRTY\_READ} & 1,416 \\
      \bottomrule
    \end{tabularx}
  \end{minipage}
  \par\vspace{1.0em}
  
  \item The missing 14.2 gigabytes of data represents critical historical records. Because the force-override command bypassed the rollback logs, these dropped records cannot be reconstructed from the destination database and are permanently lost unless recovered from physical backups.
\end{enumerate}

\par\vspace{1.0em}

% SECTION V: CONCLUSIONS
\begin{center}
  \textbf{\textcolor{LegalNavy}{\MakeUppercase{V. Expert Opinions and Conclusions}}}
\end{center}
\par\vspace{0.3em}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*., leftmargin=0.4in, rightmargin=0.1in, align=left]
  \setcounter{enumi}{16}
  \item Based on the forensic evidence, it is my professional opinion that the critical data loss suffered by Synapse was a direct result of the unilateral decision to execute the \texttt{SYS\_FORCE\_BYPASS} command. The loss of data was not caused by a hardware failure, network outage, or software bug, but rather by manual administrative intervention.
  
  \item Standard industry practices for database migrations strictly prohibit the use of bypass overrides in production environments. The execution of the force-override command was a clear deviation from standard protocols and directly violated Section 4.2 of the Migration Protocol Agreement.
\end{enumerate}

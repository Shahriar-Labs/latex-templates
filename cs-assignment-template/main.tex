\prob{1: Recurrence Relations \& the Master Theorem}

Consider the recurrence \(T(n) = 2\,T(n/2) + n \log_2 n\) with base case
\(T(1) = 1\). Determine the asymptotic behavior of \(T(n)\).

\medskip
\textbf{Solution.} We apply the Master Theorem. The recurrence has the form
\(T(n) = a\,T(n/b) + f(n)\) with \(a = 2\), \(b = 2\), and
\(f(n) = n \log_2 n\).

The critical exponent is \(c_{\text{crit}} = \log_b a = \log_2 2 = 1\).
Comparing \(f(n) = n \log_2 n\) with \(n^{c_{\text{crit}}} = n\):
\[
\frac{f(n)}{n^{c_{\text{crit}}}} = \frac{n \log_2 n}{n} = \log_2 n
\]
This grows slower than any \(n^\varepsilon\) for \(\varepsilon > 0\), yet
faster than a constant. This places us in \textbf{Case~2} of the Master
Theorem (extended form): when
\(f(n) = \Theta\bigl(n^{\log_b a} \log^k n\bigr)\) with \(k \ge 0\), we have
\[
T(n) = \Theta\bigl(n^{\log_b a} \log^{k+1} n\bigr).
\]
Here \(k = 1\), so
\[
\boxed{T(n) = \Theta\bigl(n \log^2 n\bigr)}.
\]

\begin{rvremark}
  The recurrence \(T(n) = 2\,T(n/2) + n \log n\) arises in the analysis of
  certain divide-and-conquer sorting networks and in the expected-time
  analysis of randomized quicksort with a median-of-three pivot strategy.
\end{rvremark}

\prob{2: Closest Pair of Points}

Given a set \(P = \{p_1, \dots, p_n\}\) of \(n\) points in \(\R^2\), design
a divide-and-conquer algorithm to find the closest pair under the Euclidean
distance \(d(p,q) = \|p - q\|_2\). Your algorithm must run in
\(\bigO{n \log n}\) time.

\medskip
\textbf{Solution.} The algorithm proceeds in three phases:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Pre-sort.} Build two sorted copies of \(P\): \(P_x\) sorted
    by \(x\)-coordinate and \(P_y\) sorted by \(y\)-coordinate. This costs
    \(\bigO{n \log n}\).
  \item \textbf{Divide.} Find the median \(x\)-coordinate \(x_m\) and
    partition \(P\) into the left half \(P_L\) and right half \(P_R\). Also
    split \(P_y\) into \(P_{y,L}\) and \(P_{y,R}\) while preserving the
    \(y\)-sorted order.
  \item \textbf{Conquer \& Combine.} Recursively compute
    \(\delta_L = \textsc{ClosestPair}(P_L)\) and
    \(\delta_R = \textsc{ClosestPair}(P_R)\). Let
    \(\delta = \min(\delta_L, \delta_R)\). Build the \emph{strip}
    \(S = \{\,p \in P_y \mid |x(p) - x_m| < \delta \,\}\) and check every
    point in \(S\) against the next 7 points in \(y\)-order. Return the
    minimum distance found.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{ClosestPair($P_x$, $P_y$)}
\KwIn{Sets $P_x$, $P_y$ of points sorted by $x$ and $y$, respectively.}
\KwOut{Minimum Euclidean distance between any two distinct points in $P$.}
\If{$|P_x| \le 3$}{
  Compute all pairwise distances by brute force\;
  \Return the minimum\;
}
Let $x_m \gets$ median $x$-coordinate of $P_x$\;
Partition $P$ into $P_L$, $P_R$ about the vertical line $x = x_m$\;
Build $P_{y,L}$ and $P_{y,R}$ from $P_y$ accordingly\;
$\delta_L \gets \textsc{ClosestPair}(P_{x,L},\, P_{y,L})$\;
$\delta_R \gets \textsc{ClosestPair}(P_{x,R},\, P_{y,R})$\;
$\delta \gets \min(\delta_L,\; \delta_R)$\;
$S \gets \{\, p \in P_y \mid |x(p) - x_m| < \delta \,\}$ \Comment{the strip}
\For{$i \gets 1$ \KwTo $|S|$}{
  \For{$j \gets i+1$ \KwTo $\min(i+7,\; |S|)$}{
    $\delta \gets \min(\delta,\; d(S[i],\, S[j]))$\;
  }
}
\Return $\delta$\;
\end{algorithm}

\begin{proof}[Correctness of the combine step]
  The only pairs that could beat \(\delta\) are those where one point lies in
  \(P_L\) and the other in \(P_R\) \emph{and} both lie within distance
  \(\delta\) of the split line \(x = x_m\). Every such pair belongs to the
  strip \(S\).

  Sort \(S\) by \(y\)-coordinate. For any point \(p \in S\), consider a
  \(2\delta \times \delta\) rectangle whose bottom edge passes through
  \(p\). This rectangle can contain at most 8 points (4 per side of the
  split) without any two being closer than \(\delta\). Hence checking the
  next 7 points in \(y\)-order suffices to find the closest split pair.
  The recurrence
  \[
  T(n) = 2\,T(n/2) + \bigO{n}
  \]
  solves to \(T(n) = \bigO{n \log n}\).
\end{proof}

\prob{3: Longest Common Subsequence (LCS)}

Given two strings \(X = x_1 x_2 \dots x_m\) and \(Y = y_1 y_2 \dots y_n\)
over an alphabet \(\Sigma\), find the length of the longest subsequence
common to both strings. A subsequence need not consist of contiguous
characters.

\medskip
\textbf{(a) Recurrence.} Let \(\mathrm{LCS}(i,j)\) denote the LCS length for
the prefixes \(X[1 \dots i]\) and \(Y[1 \dots j]\). Then
\[
\mathrm{LCS}(i,j) =
\begin{cases}
  0 & \text{if } i = 0 \text{ or } j = 0,\\[3pt]
  \mathrm{LCS}(i-1,\, j-1) + 1 & \text{if } x_i = y_j,\\[3pt]
  \max\bigl(\mathrm{LCS}(i-1,\,j),\;\mathrm{LCS}(i,\,j-1)\bigr)
    & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\]

\medskip
\textbf{(b) Implementation.} The DP solution fills an \((m+1) \times (n+1)\)
table bottom-up in \(\bigO{mn}\) time.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Bottom-up DP for LCS length and reconstruction.}]
def lcs(X: str, Y: str) -> tuple[int, str]:
    m, n = len(X), len(Y)
    dp = [[0] * (n + 1) for _ in range(m + 1)]

    for i in range(1, m + 1):
        for j in range(1, n + 1):
            if X[i - 1] == Y[j - 1]:
                dp[i][j] = dp[i - 1][j - 1] + 1
            else:
                dp[i][j] = max(dp[i - 1][j], dp[i][j - 1])

    # Reconstruct one LCS
    i, j = m, n
    seq = []
    while i > 0 and j > 0:
        if X[i - 1] == Y[j - 1]:
            seq.append(X[i - 1])
            i -= 1; j -= 1
        elif dp[i - 1][j] >= dp[i][j - 1]:
            i -= 1
        else:
            j -= 1

    return dp[m][n], ''.join(reversed(seq))
\end{lstlisting}

\medskip
\textbf{(c) Worked Example.} Let \(X = \texttt{AGGTAB}\) and
\(Y = \texttt{GXTXAYB}\). The DP table is:

\medskip
{\centering
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.25}
\begin{tabular}{c|cccccccc}
\toprule
      &     & G & X & T & X & A & Y & B \\
\midrule
      & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
A     & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\
G     & 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\
G     & 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\
T     & 0 & 1 & 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 & 2 & 2 \\
A     & 0 & 1 & 1 & 2 & 2 & 3 & 3 & 3 \\
B     & 0 & 1 & 1 & 2 & 2 & 3 & 3 & \cellcolor{accent!15}{\color{accent}\bfseries 4} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\par}

\medskip
The LCS length is~4; one LCS is \texttt{GTAB} (traced by following the
highlighted cell back through matches). Time complexity is
\(\bigO{mn} = \bigO{42}\) for \(|X|=6\), \(|Y|=7\); space can be reduced
to \(\bigO{\min(m,n)}\) by storing only two rows.

\prob{4: Single-Source Shortest Paths}

Consider the directed graph \(G = (V,E)\) shown below with non-negative
edge weights. Run \textbf{Dijkstra's algorithm} starting from node~\(S\).

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[

  edge/.style={-{Stealth[length=6pt]},thick,color=dark!70},
  label/.style={font=\footnotesize\color{dark},fill=white,inner sep=1pt},
  scale=1.1

  \node[node] (S) at (0,2)   {S};
  \node[node] (A) at (2,4)   {A};
  \node[node] (B) at (2,0)   {B};
  \node[node] (C) at (4,3)   {C};
  \node[node] (D) at (4,1)   {D};
  \node[node] (T) at (6,2)   {T};

  \draw[edge] (S) -- (A) node[label,midway,above left] {7};
  \draw[edge] (S) -- (B) node[label,midway,below left] {3};
  \draw[edge] (A) -- (C) node[label,midway,above]     {2};
  \draw[edge] (A) -- (D) node[label,midway,right]     {4};
  \draw[edge] (B) -- (A) node[label,midway,left]      {1};
  \draw[edge] (B) -- (D) node[label,midway,below]     {6};
  \draw[edge] (C) -- (T) node[label,midway,above right] {5};
  \draw[edge] (D) -- (C) node[label,midway,left]      {1};
  \draw[edge] (D) -- (T) node[label,midway,below right] {8};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\medskip
\textbf{Solution.} We maintain a min-priority queue of \((d[v], v)\) pairs
and relax edges as we extract the minimum. The state at each iteration:

\medskip
{\centering
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabular}{ccll}
\toprule
\textbf{Step} & \textbf{Extract} & \multicolumn{2}{c}{\textbf{Distances} \((d[S],\, d[A],\, d[B],\, d[C],\, d[D],\, d[T])\)} \\
\midrule
Init  & ---    & \((0,\) & \(\infty,\; \infty,\; \infty,\; \infty,\; \infty)\) \\
1     & \(S\)  & \((0,\) & \(7,\;           3,\;           \infty,\; \infty,\; \infty)\) \\
2     & \(B\)  & \((0,\) & \(4,\;           3,\;           \infty,\; 9,\;            \infty)\) \\
3     & \(A\)  & \((0,\) & \(4,\;           3,\;           6,\;           8,\;            \infty)\) \\
4     & \(C\)  & \((0,\) & \(4,\;           3,\;           6,\;           8,\;            11)\) \\
5     & \(D\)  & \((0,\) & \(4,\;           3,\;           6,\;           7,\;            11)\) \\
6     & \(T\)  & \((0,\) & \(4,\;           3,\;           6,\;           7,\;            11)\) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\par}

\medskip
The shortest-path distances from \(S\) are:

\medskip
{\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}c|c|c|c|c|c@{}}
\toprule
\(S\) & \(A\) & \(B\) & \(C\) & \(D\) & \(T\) \\
\midrule
0 & 4 & 3 & 6 & 7 & 11 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\par}

\medskip
The shortest path \(S \to T\) is \(S \to B \to A \to C \to T\) with total
weight \(3 + 1 + 2 + 5 = 11\). Alternative route \(S \to B \to D \to C \to T\)
has weight \(3 + 6 + 1 + 5 = 15\), confirming Dijkstra's correctness on
this instance.
